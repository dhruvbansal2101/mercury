## The Core Problem

You have **two competing systems**:
- **Nav2** — thinks in terms of "get from A to B, avoid obstacles"
- **Lane detection** — thinks in terms of "stay centered between these white lines"

Nav2 doesn't know lanes exist. It just sees free space on the costmap. So when you give it a goal 20m away on a U-track, it computes the shortest collision-free path — which might cut across the inside of the U if the costmap doesn't perfectly represent the lane boundaries.

Your lane_assist_node is only a **correction layer** on top of Nav2's `cmd_vel` — it nudges the angular velocity slightly. But Nav2's DWB controller is also commanding angular velocity, and DWB's PathAlign and PathDist critics (scaled at 32.0 in your config) will fight back against that correction, especially on curves where the planned path is already curving hard.

---

## The Different Approaches

### Approach 1 — Lane Costmap (inject lanes into Nav2's world)

Convert your lane detection output into a `nav2_costmap_2d` layer. Mark the area **outside** the detected lane boundaries as high-cost or lethal. Now Nav2's planner naturally routes along the lane because going outside it looks expensive on the costmap.

**In your repo:** You already have `/perception/road_costmap` referenced in `bringup.rviz` and the `lane_costmap` node referenced in `watchdog_monitor` expected nodes list — but that node doesn't exist yet. This slot is already reserved in your architecture.

**Pros:** Works with any goal distance. Nav2 plans correctly on its own. Lane following and obstacle avoidance are unified. The most "correct" professional approach.

**Cons:** Lane detection needs to be reliable enough to produce a clean costmap. Flicker in detection = flickering costmap = Nav2 replanning constantly.

---

### Approach 2 — Waypoint Snapping to Lane Center

Instead of giving Nav2 a single far goal, you generate a sequence of short intermediate goals that are explicitly placed at the **lane center** at regular intervals ahead of the robot. These come from your lane detection — you know where the lane center is in the image, you convert that to a world-frame pose using the camera transform, and place the next waypoint there.

**In your repo:** Your `goal_decomposer` currently samples from Nav2's planned path. You could instead generate waypoints directly from lane detection output — every time lane center is detected with high confidence, project it forward 2–3m and drop a waypoint there.

**Pros:** Doesn't depend on the costmap having perfect lane representation. Works even if the map is incomplete.

**Cons:** Short lookahead means the robot can't see around corners. If lane detection fails, navigation stops. Needs camera-to-world projection which requires knowing the camera's transform precisely.

---

### Approach 3 — Override DWB with a Custom Controller

Replace DWB with a custom `nav2_controller` plugin that directly uses `/lane_center_error` as its primary steering signal. Nav2 still handles goal management and lifecycle, but your lane controller drives.

**In your repo:** Would require a new C++ or Python controller plugin. Significant work. But the cleanest integration — Nav2 manages "which goal to go to" and your controller manages "how to drive there."

**Pros:** Full authority. Lane following is the primary loop, not a correction.

**Cons:** Most complex to implement. Need to handle all edge cases DWB handles for free.

---

### Approach 4 — Reduce DWB Authority, Increase Lane Assist Authority

This is the simplest fix for your immediate problem. In `nav2_params.yaml`, dramatically reduce `PathAlign.scale` and `PathDist.scale` (from 32.0 to ~5.0), and increase `Kp` in `lane_assist_node`. DWB still handles obstacle avoidance and gross direction but lane assist has more authority over steering.

**Pros:** Zero new code. Can test immediately.

**Cons:** Fragile. You're fighting two controllers rather than unifying them. Unpredictable on tight turns.

---

### Approach 5 — Segments-Based Navigation (Most Practical for UGVC-10)

Pre-define the track as a series of **named segments** with known geometry (straight, left-turn, right-turn, U-turn). For each segment type, you switch between different control modes — pure lane following on straights, Nav2 waypoint navigation on turns. A state machine manages transitions.

**In your repo:** `watchdog_monitor/waypoint_detector_node.py` already has the waypoint-detection infrastructure. You could use it to detect when the robot enters a turn segment and switch modes.

**Pros:** Very reliable because each segment is handled by the best tool for it. Easy to tune per-segment.

**Cons:** Requires you to know the track layout in advance. Not fully autonomous in the "works on any track" sense.

The Core Problem
Your current lane_detection.py (v4) uses Hough lines on a white mask directly on the camera image with an ROI crop. The fragmentation you're seeing happens because:

Perspective distortion — at track curves, one lane line appears much closer/farther
Lane width assumption is wrong — the lane_half_width_px parameter is calibrated for a specific camera height and angle, but curves break this assumption
The BEV (bird's eye view) approach is actually more robust for lane width estimation


Approaches
Approach 1: Fix the existing Hough-on-ROI (current approach)
Tune thresholds, use RANSAC instead of basic Hough, better morphological ops. Problem: Fragmentation on curves is fundamental to perspective projection — patching won't fully solve it.
Approach 2: Proper BEV (Bird's Eye View) + Hough
Warp the image to top-down view using a homography calibrated to your specific camera mount, then run lane detection on the flat BEV. Why better: Lane lines are parallel and constant-width in BEV regardless of curves. Problem: Needs careful homography calibration for your exact camera pose (xyz="0.15 0 2" rpy="0 0.4 0").
Approach 3: BEV + Sliding Window (like Udacity-style lane detection)
After BEV warp → white mask → sliding window histogram search bottom-up. This is the most robust approach used in real AV systems. Handles curves naturally, gives you a polynomial fit of the lane, and gives you actual lane center not just a pixel offset.
Approach 4: Pure Color Segmentation + Contour Fitting
Skip edges entirely, use color mask → find two largest vertical blobs → fit lines. Simpler but fragile in varying lighting.

My Recommendation
Approach 3: BEV + Sliding Window — here's why it wins for your case:

Your camera is already mounted high (z=2m) and tilted, which is ideal for BEV
Sliding window naturally handles curves — it follows the lane as it curves
You get a polynomial fit → lane center is geometrically correct even on tight turns
No lane width assumption needed — you measure actual pixel separation
Works in one go once calibrated

The key question is: what does your camera actually see in simulation? The homography needs to map the trapezoid of visible road to a rectangle. With xyz="0.15 0 2" rpy="0 0.4 0" (2m height, 0.4 rad forward tilt ≈ 23°), you have a decent FOV of the road ahead.
